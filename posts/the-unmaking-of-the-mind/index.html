The Unmaking of the Mind

05 May 2026

Ver em Português

tl;dr: This is a very long article, written in a very different style for me. Forgive any imbalances here.

There is a strange, unspoken feeling many of us carry now. You sit down to read something longer than a few paragraphs. A book, an article, maybe this one. And within a minute or two, your hand drifts toward your phone. Not because you heard a notification. Not because the writing is bad. Just because your attention has been trained, over years of small habits, to expect a break every few seconds. You check your screen. Nothing new. You already knew that. You do it anyway

I caught myself doing this yesterday. I was reading a short story I had been waiting all week to read. Three paragraphs in, I was scrolling Instagram. I don’t remember opening the app.

This is not a personal failing. It is a psychic restructuring.

Over the past twenty five years, few forces in history have altered the very architecture of human interiority. Not just what we think, but how we think. Not just what we feel, but whether we can feel it all the way through. The internet has delivered undeniable goods: access to knowledge, connection across distances, voice to the silenced. But those goods have come at a price that we are only beginning to inventory. This article is that inventory. It will not be balanced. It will argue that the harm outweighs the good, and that the human psyche, in its collective texture, is now shallower, more reactive, more ashamed, and more lonely than it was before the world went online.

The Scattering of the Self

One of the first casualties was duration. Human attention, before the internet, was not a pristine Eden. People have always daydreamed, procrastinated, wandered. But it was capable of sustained immersion. You could read a novel for three hours. You could have a conversation that lasted an evening. You could be bored, and in that boredom something would eventually emerge: an idea, a memory, a piece of inner furniture you had forgotten existed.

Boredom, as the psychoanalyst Adam Phillips once noted, is the mind’s way of telling itself that something is missing. That idea is worth holding onto. Boredom is a necessary discomfort, a gestalt switch waiting to happen. The internet has severely eroded boredom. In its place: infinite microstimulation. A scroll, a swipe, a cut, a laugh, a hot take, a tragedy, a recipe, another swipe. The gaps between stimuli have shrunk to milliseconds. The result is not happiness but fraying.

The neuroscience here is straightforward. Constant task switching trains the brain to release dopamine not for completion or insight, but for novelty. The cycle becomes self reinforcing: the more you switch, the harder it becomes to stay; the harder it becomes to stay, the more you switch. Over time, the default mode network (the brain system associated with self reflection, memory consolidation, and long term planning) becomes underused. You lose the ability to be alone with your own thoughts because, quite literally, those thoughts no longer gather into a coherent stream. They become a feed.

This is not metaphor. A 2018 Stanford study found that heavy multitaskers had more difficulty filtering irrelevant information and took longer to switch between tasks. Their brains had adapted to a rhythm of constant interruption. The mind becomes a browser with forty seven tabs open, none of them fully loaded, none of them closed.

Yes, the internet allows unprecedented access to information. You can learn calculus or ancient history or how to fix a dishwasher in ten minutes. But access without absorption is not knowledge. It is reference. The difference between knowing something and being able to look it up is the difference between having a skeleton and having a phone number for a skeleton. One holds you upright. The other is just a call away, until the battery dies.

The Outrage Pendulum

Feelings have duration. Grief, for example, is a slow withdrawal of psychic energy from a lost object. It takes time. It takes quiet. It takes the freedom not to immediately express every flicker of feeling.

The internet does not allow this. It transforms emotions into events. You feel a flash of anger at a headline. Before you can ask yourself whether the anger is justified, or whether it is yours or someone else’s, you have already typed a reply. The reply gets likes. The likes reinforce the anger. The anger hardens into identity. What might have been a passing irritation becomes a permanent feature of your online self, and then, because there is no wall between online and offline, a permanent feature of your actual self.

Regulation requires pause. The internet has eliminated pause at the architectural level. Algorithms are not neutral; they are optimized for arousal, not well being. A 2021 study from MIT found that angry or outraged content spreads significantly faster than neutral or positive content. The result is a collective nervous system stuck in low grade fight or flight, scrolling through catastrophe after catastrophe, each new horror demanding a response before the last one has been metabolized.

Jung’s work on psychological types is useful here. He described the feeling function and the thinking function as two different ways of processing. Emotion without reflection becomes possession. The internet is a possession machine. It inflates feeling while starving the reflective pause that would allow feeling to be integrated rather than acted out.

And what of joy? What of quiet contentment? These are not rewarded. A photo of a sunset gets a fraction of the engagement of a political flamewar. The psyche learns, operantly, that intensity is currency. Over time, you become incapable of small pleasures. You need hotter takes, louder conflicts, sharper betrayals. This is not moral failure. It is neurological conditioning.

The Performance of the Self

Developmental psychology has long understood that identity is formed through a process of synthesis. A person must integrate their various roles (child, friend, student, lover, citizen) into a coherent whole. This synthesis happens in private, through trial, contradiction, and revision. You try on a self, see how it feels when no one is watching, and then adjust.

Social media reversed this process. Identity now is formed in public, from the outside in. You do not ask: Who am I? You ask: What will get likes? The difference is not trivial. It is the difference between sculpture and plaster cast. One emerges from within; the other is pressed into a mold.

The result is a deep fragmentation that looks like coherence. A person has a LinkedIn self (competent, ambitious), an Instagram self (beautiful, spontaneous), a Twitter self (angry, righteous), a TikTok self (ironic, silly), and a private self that is none of these things and all of them, exhausted. The private self is where shame lives. Shame, in the psychoanalytic sense, is not just embarrassment. It is the sense that the real you is unacceptable. When every platform rewards performance over authenticity, the gap between real and performed widens until the real begins to feel unreal.

Jung’s concept of the persona (the social mask we wear to navigate the world) was always meant to be flexible, a tool, not a prison. The internet has made the persona rigid and multiple at once. You cannot drop the mask because, after a decade of curation, you are not sure there is a face underneath.

The internet has also allowed marginalized people to find communities and build identities outside oppressive local environments. A queer teenager in a small town can discover that they are not alone. This is real and valuable. But it is not free. That same teenager is also learning to perform their identity for an audience, to internalize the aesthetics and vocabulary of a global subculture, to measure their authenticity by likes. The cure is not worse than the disease, but it is not a clean cure either. It is a trade. And trades, by definition, involve loss.

The Digital Panopticon

The superego is the internalized voice of authority that judges and punishes from within. A healthy superego is strict but not tyrannical. An unhealthy one is relentless. The internet has become an external superego, a panopticon of perpetual judgment.

Before the web, shame was usually private, or at least local. You said something stupid at a party; a dozen people heard it. You apologized; you moved on. Now, a single badly worded tweet from six years ago can be screenshotted, circulated, and used as evidence of your fundamental moral failure by strangers who have never met you. The scale is inhuman. The permanence is new.

The result is a population that is simultaneously shameless (posting every thought, every photograph, every vulnerability) and hyper shame sensitive (terrified of cancellation, of missteps, of being seen as the wrong kind of person). This paradox is not a contradiction; it is a defense. You preemptively expose everything so that nothing can be used against you. You turn yourself inside out so that no hidden secret remains. But this is not liberation. It is self surveillance.

Jung’s shadow concept is essential here. The shadow is the parts of yourself that you do not want to see, that you repress but that nonetheless act upon you. The internet does not integrate the shadow; it projects it. You see in others the very vices you cannot own in yourself. The callout post, the cancellation, the righteous pile on: these are collective shadow projections, rituals of sacrifice that leave the projector feeling clean while the target is destroyed. The psyche is not healed by this. It is impoverished. The shadow grows stronger in exile.

Loneliness

There is a layer of the psyche shared by all humans, containing archetypes (the Mother, the Hero, the Trickster, the Shadow) that shape our dreams, myths, and religions. This collective unconscious is deep, slow, and ancient. It operates in symbols, not in words.

The internet has given us a counterfeit collective unconscious. We call it viral culture or the memescape or the discourse. It moves fast, not slow. It is made of fragments, not archetypes. A hashtag is not a myth. A challenge is not a ritual. A trend is not a symbol.

And yet we feel lonely. Not the loneliness of solitude, which can be generative, but the loneliness of constant ersatz connection. You can talk to a hundred people a day and never be touched. You can see the inner lives of strangers with surgical intimacy (their births, their meals, their griefs) and feel nothing but exhaustion. The collective unconscious is starved. The archetypes are still there, but they have no container. No shared stories. No silent campfires.

In 1995, a person waiting for a bus would stand and look at the sky, or at other people, or at nothing at all. That emptiness was not a problem to be solved; it was the background against which thoughts could appear. Now that background is gone. Every pause is filled.

Group psychology describes how crowds regress to a primitive state, led by a leader, bound by identification. The internet is a permanent crowd without a single leader, or rather with a thousand leaders, each demanding loyalty, each flickering in and out of relevance. The psyche cannot rest in this environment. It is always orienting, always scanning for threat and reward, always performing for an imagined audience that never leaves.

What Is Lost, Finally

I promised this article would not be balanced. Here is the imbalance. The internet has done enormous good. It has democratized knowledge, enabled organizing, connected the isolated, given voice to the silenced. None of that is erased by pointing out the damage. But damage is not erased by pointing out the benefits either.

The human psyche is not a spreadsheet. You cannot total the pros and cons. You can only ask: what kind of inner life is possible now? And the answer, for more and more people, is a thinner one. Less sustained attention. Less deep feeling. Less private identity. More shame, more performance, more outrage, more loneliness. The collective unconscious is not dead, but it is bleeding into a feed. The shadow is not integrated; it is projected onto strangers. The self is not discovered; it is optimized.

The modest goal of psychoanalysis was to replace hysterical misery with ordinary unhappiness. The internet, in its perverse way, has achieved the opposite. It has replaced ordinary unhappiness (boredom, awkwardness, the slow work of being alive) with a low grade, never satisfied psychospiritual agitation. It has made us not happier or sadder, but frayed.

You can still read a book for three hours. You can still sit in silence. You can still feel your own feelings without posting them. But you have to fight for it now. And the fight is against a machine that was designed, in every detail, to make you lose.

That is the unmaking. It is not complete yet. But it is further along than most of us want to admit.
