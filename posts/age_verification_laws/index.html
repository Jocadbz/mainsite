Big Brother wears nanny clothes

05 Mar 2026

It is impossible to deny that the internet has drastically changed since its inception in popular culture roughly in the early 2000s. In the first section of his book Cyberia, author Douglas Rushkoff closely explores early cyberculture and its users. Reading it from a modern standpoint, you might feel like you’ve stumbled upon an ancient manuscript. Cyberspaces were close-knit, intimate, and intensely private. Users rarely shared personal information, and most communicated via pseudonyms. The internet landscape was vast, and it was free. People logged on to escape real life, or perhaps to share and consume content otherwise deemed unsavory by societal standards. It was refreshing, it was private, and above all, it was truly free.

Much to my disgust - and that of other long-time users - the internet has changed entirely too much. Cyberspaces are now massive Cybercities where everyone exists on the same platform. And somehow, for some reason, people share their identities openly without a care in the world. It’s no longer rare to see profiles featuring full legal names alongside photos of homes, routines, workplaces, friends, and whatever else is going on in their daily lives.

Putting aside the terrifying implications of having a profile like that public (a subject for another post), it seems the real world has completely merged with cyberspace. Rules that were once mere suggestions are now being heavily enforced, both by the corporations controlling platform traffic and, alarmingly, by the state. Until recently, it was still possible to ignore those rules and maintain a rather strong privacy profile. But that is rapidly changing, thanks to the legislation that forms the core of this post.

The Age Verification Laws

By 2026, California and Brazil moved to the forefront of global tech regulation with two landmark laws: the Digital Age Assurance Act (AB 1043) and the Digital Statute of Children and Adolescents (Law 15,211).

In California, the Digital Age Assurance Act (AB 1043) was signed into law in late 2025 and is set to take effect on January 1, 2027. This law is unique because it shifts the responsibility of age verification from individual apps to operating system (OS) providers like Apple and Google. During a device’s initial setup, the OS must prompt the user for their birth date and then share an encrypted “age signal” (categorizing them into brackets like under 13, 13–15, etc.) with any apps they download.

In Brazil, the regulatory landscape is dominated by the Digital Statute of Children and Adolescents (Law 15,211), expected to enter into full force on March 17, 2026. This law significantly raises the bar for digital platforms, moving beyond simple “I am over 18” checkboxes to require “reasonable assurance” of age for any service likely to be accessed by minors.

Sounds well-intentioned, right? It isn’t. These laws represent perhaps the most direct legal attack on internet privacy ever conceived. Proponents hide behind the shield of “protecting children” without considering what enforcing these laws actually entails.

When an operating system shares an “encrypted signal,” politicians claim it’s a win for privacy because the specific app doesn’t see your physical ID. This is a classic bait-and-switch. By tethering a verified age bracket to a permanent hardware ID or a central Google/Apple account, we are effectively ending the era of the “burner” profile.

If every app you download - from a niche political forum to a fitness tracker - receives the same verified token from your OS, your cross-platform identity becomes unified. You aren’t just a random anonymous user anymore; you are User #88291, a confirmed 24-year-old male, verified via hardware. The “nanny” isn’t just checking your ID at the door; she’s tattooing a serial number on your arm so she can follow you from room to room.

Brazil’s Law 15,211 takes this intrusion a step further. “Reasonable assurance” is simply a legal euphemism for biometric surveillance. To comply with such high stakes, platforms are already pivoting toward third-party “Identity Providers.” Soon, to watch a video or join a chat, you’ll be prompted to scan your face or upload a national ID. This creates massive, centralized honeypots of sensitive data. History has shown us that it isn’t a matter of if these databases get hacked, but when. We are being forced to trade our most intimate biological data just to bypass a digital velvet rope, all under the guise of “protecting the children.”

The Death of the Persona

The tragedy here isn’t just about inevitable data leaks; it’s about the death of the digital persona. In the Cyberia days Rushkoff described, you could be a philosopher at 2 AM and a gamer at 4 AM, with no tether linking the two. Age verification kills the pseudonym. If the state or the OS knows exactly who is sitting behind the screen, the lack of inhibition that once allowed for radical self-expression disappears. We become performative, acutely aware that our “permanent record” is being updated in real-time.

Let’s not forget that these laws also destroy the “legal” and necessary use cases for strict privacy. Journalists, whistleblowers, and vulnerable individuals are effectively stripped of their safety nets. A journalist using a public computer will never hand over her ID just to publish a story that could put her life in danger. A whistleblower shouldn’t have to verify his identity to leak protected information, thereby defeating the entire purpose of leaking it.

We are told these laws are necessary, that they protect the vulnerable, that they are well-intentioned and good. These are lies. By forcing the “real world” into the digital one, we are losing the only space left where we could truly be anonymous. We are burning down the frontier to build a gated community - and we might find that once the gates are locked, we’re the ones trapped inside.

If you are in favor of these rules, I only ask you to think for a moment. You just gave the state a mechanism to identify everyone posting on the internet. It might sound amazing for silencing people you don’t like, but… what happens when the state decides it dislikes you?
